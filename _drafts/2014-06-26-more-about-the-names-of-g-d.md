The many laws governing the writing of G&#8209;d’s name are extremely complex and beyond the scope of this blog,[^fn1] but there are a few [hidurim]({% comment %}{% post_url 2011-12-17-purchasing-stam-7-bidieved-lechatchila-mehudar#mehudar %}{% endcomment %}) which are specific to G&#8209;d’s name and warrant special mention:

### Shemos Kisidran 
{: #shemos-kesidran}

Unlike tefilin and mezuzos, Sifrei Torah do not need to be written [kisidran](/stam-101#kisidran/). However, there is a minority opinion that *the names of G&#8209;d* must be written kisidran even in Sifrei Torah. This opinion should be taken into consideration if at all possible. 

### Consecutive *Lishem Kidushas*
{: #consecutive-lishem-kidushas}

The names of G&#8209;d require the declared intent to write “…for the sake of the holiness of the name (of G&#8209;d)”. When multiple instances of G&#8209;d’s name appear consecutively, one declaration is enough for all of them. There are poskim who say that it is preferable for consecutive Shemos to all get their *own* declaration, and although it is not a requirement, it is recommended to do so.

### Expanded List of Safek Shemos 
{: #expanded-list-safek-shemos}

Most of the references to G&#8209;d in the Torah are unambiguous and must be written lishma. There are also words that *might* be G&#8209;d’s name and they must be written conditionally-lishma. Then there are words which are the subject of a dispute – some Poskim say they are the name of G&#8209;d (and must be written lishma), some say they are *not* the name of G&#8209;d (and may *not* be written lishma), and some may even say they should be written conditionally-lishma. In deciding how to treat these disputed words, many sofrim follow a “standard” list which is based on only one opinion – *and it is not necessarily the majority opinion*. There are other lists available which take into account a wider variety of sources, including a comprehensive list based on the rulings of Rav Y. S. Elyashiv. Following one of the expanded lists when writing a Sefer Torah would add to the hidur of the Sefer.
